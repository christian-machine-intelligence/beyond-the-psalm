Geography of Iceland

Iceland is a Nordic island country located in the North Atlantic Ocean, situated at the junction of the Mid-Atlantic Ridge and the Greenland-Iceland-Scottish transatlantic ridge. The island lies between latitudes 63 and 66 degrees north, and longitudes 13 and 24 degrees west. Its total area is approximately 103,000 square kilometers, making it the eighteenth largest island in the world and the second largest island in Europe after Great Britain.

The geological composition of Iceland is predominantly basaltic, owing to its position on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, where the Eurasian and North American tectonic plates diverge. This divergence produces significant volcanic and geothermal activity throughout the island. The country contains approximately 130 volcanic mountains, of which around 30 are considered active. The most prominent volcanic systems include Hekla, Katla, Grimsvotn, and the Eyjafjallajokull system, which gained international attention following its 2010 eruption that disrupted air travel across northern Europe.

The landscape of Iceland is characterized by a central highland plateau consisting of sand and lava fields, mountains, and glaciers. Approximately 11 percent of the country is covered by glaciers, the largest of which is Vatnajokull, covering roughly 8,100 square kilometers. Vatnajokull is the largest glacier in Europe by volume and the second largest by area. Other significant glaciers include Langjokull, Hofsjokull, and Myrdalsjokull, which caps the Katla volcano.

Iceland's coastline extends approximately 4,970 kilometers and is characterized by numerous fjords, particularly along the northern and eastern coasts. The Westfjords peninsula in the northwest is deeply indented with fjords and represents some of the oldest geological formations on the island, dating back approximately 14 to 16 million years. The southern coast, by contrast, tends to be flatter, with extensive black sand beaches formed from eroded volcanic material.

The country's river systems are fed primarily by glacial meltwater and precipitation. The longest river is the Thjorsa, extending approximately 230 kilometers from its source near the Hofsjokull glacier to its mouth on the southern coast. Other major rivers include the Jokulsa a Fjollum, which flows northward from the Vatnajokull glacier, and the Hvita, known for its association with the Gullfoss waterfall.
